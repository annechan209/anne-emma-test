    More details

    Dominic is coming to the end of his second term in prison. He’s 32 and has been in prison, in Armley, for nearly 4 years. After his first spell in prison, he applied for Universal Credit. He remembers the stress of being released last time; everything he had to line up in that first week, and it was still ages until his money came through. It seemed like such a hassle that he started dealing straight away. Within a year, he was in prison again.

    Devices and technology

    Dominic doesn’t have a phone or a laptop at the moment. He wants to sort out a phone when he gets out, but at the moment he only has access to a computer during certain hours of the day. It very restricted, and most of the time he doesn’t bother trying to use it. He recently applied for UC with a support worker inside the prison. During the application, she asked him questions and typed everything in for him. He wouldn’t feel confident doing the form himself and gets frustrated when he doesn’t understand how he’s supposed to answer.

    Goals and wishes

    Dominic isn’t sure what he wants to do when he gets out. He’s going to have to live with his mum for a while and knows that she’ll be on his case about getting a job. He’s moving out of Leeds to live with her and hopes that it will help him avoid the people he used to hang around with.

    He’s sick of being locked up, and feels like he’s had a run of bad luck which has cost him a lot of years. He doesn’t want to get into drugs again, it’s too risky. He learnt a bit of bricklaying in prison. Hopefully it will lead to work, but he doesn’t know where to begin.

    Frustrations

    Dominic knows that it is going to be hard when he’s released. He’s got a lot to do and he hates admin and the feeling that he’s being bounced around by the system. He thinks he’s calmed down a bit, but can still kick off when he feels messed about. He thinks that the safest thing for him is to take things slowly, and try not to do too much, too soon.

    His key worker in the prison has said that he might not need to go to the jobcentre when he’s released, because of coronavirus. His benefits still feel a bit vague. He knows that the application isn’t finished yet, but doesn’t know what he will have to do to make sure the money comes in.

    He might have to go to a jobcentre near his mum’s, in Redcar. He hasn’t been there before. He’s got a bank account, but hasn’t used it in ages. He’s got a passport and driving licence. They are probably at his mum’s house, but he doesn’t know if they are still in date.

    Making things better for Dominic

    Issue What to do
    Dominic is in prison. He doesn’t have access to personal documents which he may be able to use during an online IDV journey. Understand all of the options that may allow Dominic’s ID to be confirmed before he is released, possibly drawing on prison documentation.
    Dominic knows that the first week on release is going to be difficult and stressful. He doesn’t want his first week on release to be highly pressured Connect with the Prison Leavers Project to understand the contact points that Dominic is likely to have in his first week, and understand the ways in which DWP identity services can help to reduce unnecessary contact.
    Dominic already has support in prison, but they are currently unable to help him through his whole UC application. Explore options to move the entire UC claim online, and how ID verification services can contribute to this.
    Dominic doesn’t have access to a smart phone or laptop outside prison. Connect online and offline journeys, combining online application and offline support to ensure an end-to-end journey which can respond to user-needs in different phases of their claim and maintenance of claim.
    Dominic has been in the prison system for the long time, and the burden is still on him to prove his identity. Explore options to utilise documents and data for IDV. Including data held through the conviction and sentencing lifecycle.
